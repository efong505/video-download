**Do I Really Need to Learn AI… or Will It Leave Me Behind? My Personal Wake-Up Call**

As a cloud-native platform architect and someone who just finished a six-year grind earning both a bachelor’s and master’s degree in Information Technology the old-fashioned way — no shortcuts, just hard work and late nights — I never expected AI to become the biggest turning point in my career.

But it did.

In the fall of 2025 I sat down to build ChristianConservativesToday.com completely from scratch. I wanted a fast, secure, censorship-resistant platform for faith-driven news, ministry resources, and community. Traditionally, a project like that would have taken a small team many months. Instead, I decided to test the waters with AI.

What happened next shocked me.

Within weeks I had a fully functional web platform with serverless architecture, Angular front-end, .NET back-end, database schemas, and automated deployment pipelines. The productivity gain felt like discovering gold. I started calling the AI my “senile Einstein” — brilliant beyond measure, capable of generating elaborate, sophisticated code in seconds… but then completely forgetting what it had just written in the next conversation.

That experience opened my eyes. AI wasn’t just a helpful tool. It was something far more powerful — and far more dangerous — than most people realize.

**The Double Danger: Underestimating or Overestimating AI**

Right now, people fall into one of two traps:

1. **They underestimate AI** — They think it’s just a fancy search engine or a novelty that writes emails. They don’t understand its real capabilities or how fast it’s already reshaping every industry.

2. **They overestimate AI** — They blindly trust it, copy-pasting its output without discernment, ignoring hallucinations, built-in biases, and the long-term safety implications of handing over more and more decisions to machines.

Both attitudes are dangerous.

In my own journey I saw both sides clearly. I watched AI dramatically accelerate my work, yet I also witnessed its quirks, its ideological leanings, and its potential to replace entire roles — even for people with degrees and decades of experience.

That realization hit hard:  
A less-experienced person with no degree but who knows how to use AI effectively could now outperform someone like me who had spent years in formal education and hands-on systems engineering. The playing field wasn’t just leveling — it was tilting in favor of those who mastered the tool.

That tension is exactly why I titled the book **The Necessary Evil**.

I didn’t want to use AI. I felt reluctant. But ignoring it wasn’t an option either. If I refused to learn it, I risked being left behind. If I used it carelessly, I risked losing the very human dignity and stewardship God calls us to exercise.

So instead of waiting to see what would happen, I did a deep dive. I researched current uses of AI across every major industry, the real upsides, the hidden downsides, the good, the bad, and the ugly. Out of that honest examination, this book was born.

**Why This Matters for Every Person in Every Field**

AI isn’t coming for “tech jobs” only. It’s coming for **all** jobs.

From healthcare and education to manufacturing, law, finance, ministry, creative work, and even skilled trades — no industry is untouched. Some roles will be replaced. Others will be transformed. The people who thrive will be those who learn to master AI as a tool rather than letting it master them.
